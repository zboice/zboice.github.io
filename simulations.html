This video shows one example of geodesic motion in the Schwarzschild spacetime geometry, i.e. the line element reads $$ ds^2 = f dt^2 + f^{-1} dr^2 + r^2 (d\theta^2 + \sin \theta d\varphi^2) .$$ We choose the particle to be confined to the equatorial plane ($\theta = \pi/2$) and assume that the radial position of the test mass is given by $$ r_p(\chi) = \frac{p M}{1 + e \cos(\chi)} $$ where $e$ and $p$ are the eccentricity and semi-latus rectum, respectively. The variable $\chi$ is the relativistic anomaly and allows us to appropriately parameterize orbits (relativistic orbits don't necessarily close on themselves after one full cycle). With $\chi$ defined, we are able to formulate differential equations for $\frac{d t}{d\chi}$ and $\frac{d \varphi_p}{d \chi}$ (see Martel for more information). Integrating these equations with RK4 provides all the information we desire about the system.

This video shows the motion of a test mass in a gravitational potential well (centered at the origin). The three different orbits come from using different methods to numerically integrate the system of differential equations that defines the motion. RK23 and LSODA are two common integrators with different degrees of accuracy (the less accurate RK23 method diverges very quickly). The symplectic integrator is based off of Kessels' paper and has a lower degree of accuracy than the LSODA method. However, symplectic methods are built to preserve a deeper structure of phase space (the $(q, p)$) plane, which roughly translates a method that conserves energy more closely. For a fixed time step across methods, the symplectic method most accurately gives the elliptical orbit we expect. Go here to learn more.
